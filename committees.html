FINANCIAL STABILITY BOARD


SIZE: 40-45 members and provisional members

DIRECTORS: Amudhini RA, Vartika Neeraj
The Financial Stability Board (FSB) is an international body that monitors and makes recommendations about the global financial system. It was established after the 2009 G-20 London summit in April 2009 as a successor to the Financial Stability Forum (FSF). The Board includes all G-20 major economies, FSF members, and the European Commission. It is based in Basel, Switzerland.
The FSB being stimulated at SANSKRITI MUN’2016 would be the perfect nexus of technical knowledge, nimbleness, and analytical skills. The agendas being stimulated pertain to current issues that are of utmost global importance and have a severe debilitating effect on the economies of all the member states.

1. Transforming Shadow Banking into resilient market-based finance: Shadow Banking System' refers to the financial intermediaries involved in facilitating the creation of credit across the global financial system, but whose members are not subject to regulatory oversight. The shadow banking system also refers to unregulated activities by regulated institutions. The committee would discuss matters like the persisting detrimental effects of shadow banking and how it impacted the financial crisis (2007-12),strengthening the oversight and regulation of shadow banking, effectiveness of previous policies and propose new policies for tackling the problems arisen by shadow banking.

2. Regulating the Impact of Economic Warfare on Developing Countries: Economic warfare includes the ability to exert influence in the way of another nation’s global stance with respect to reducing its military power and political stability by the usage of economic mediums. It includes sanctions, embargoes, restriction of capital inflow, change in tariff rates etc. Being viewed as an alternative to military usage it has severe economic impacts on all nations especially those which do not have a stable and growing economy. With sanctions on Iran crippling the nation’s oil dependant economy and the Russian rubles daily fluctuations, economic warfare has become the weapon of selection and in today’s time it is imperative to discuss this issue on a global platform. This committee would act as a rostrum for the regulation of the same paving way to the construction of comprehensive international dictum for the establishment of a substantial law to rule such fundamental economics of supply and demand.

THE MUGHAL COURT OF 1615


SPECIALIZED



DIRECTORS:Jailekha Zutshi, Apoorv Pandey

Committee Size: 15-20

The year is 1615. The place is Agra, North India.

In recent years, the Mughal Empire has grown in size and power under Jahangir and his predecessor Akbar. Yet, despite its seemingly formidable exterior, the ill effects of rapid expansion are starting to show. Rogue provinces such as Mewar may finally be capitulating, but the details of their currently dubious alliance still have to be cemeneted. Meanwhile, Sir Thomas Roe, the ambassador of the court of King James I of England to Jahangir’s court, has finally arrived at Agra, creating a stir both within the Emperor’s retinue and between the Jesuit priests from Portugal present in court. While Roe’s mission lies in ensuring protection for the East India Company’s factory in Surat, he seems adamant on becoming a favourite of the Emperor, much to the chagrin of the latter’s family.

Meanwhile, within the borders of the empire, a plethora of problems merits urgent attention. Though Khusrau, Jahangir’s eldest son, was blinded after a failed attempt to seize power in 1607; Prince Khurram, the third eldest of the emperors sons, has maintained a cool distance since Jahangir married Nur Jahan in 1611. Malik Ambar, a rebel leading the Deccan against the might of the Empire, has lain low for a time, but may strike at any time, making the status of recent victories in the area uncertain. Moreover, a defining battle with the empire’s long-time enemy the Raja of Kangra is imminent, as is guerilla warfare with the Ahoms of Burma. In addition, expanding the empire and simultaneously appeasing a large and diverse population remain important concerns.

The future of the Mughal Empire lies in your hands. Together, attacks must be planned, traitors must be punished and alliances must be forged if the Empire is to retain its glory.

“One of the critical issues that we have to confront is illegal immigration, because this is a multi- headed Hydra that affects our economy, our health care, our education systems, our national security, and also our local criminality.” -Allen West

“We cannot continue doing the same thing and expect different results” – Otto Pérez Molina, President of Guatemala on the War on Drugs

Illegal Migration and Drug Trafficking have been contentious issues in the North and South American states for years. Drug Trafficking is one of the most lucrative businesses in Mexico, United States of America and Colombia due to the convenient location of the countries. Illegal Migration to the United States has been one of the highly debated topics for the US Presidential Elections in 2016.

According to Amnesty International, workers crossing the border into America are routinely kidnapped, raped, and murdered by local gangs. The authorities respond by doing nothing. By Amnesty’s estimates, this indifference results in tens of thousands of deaths and assaults every year. Migrants from Mexico face the remote wastes of the burning Arizona desert—an area known as the Corridor of Death. Since 2001, this stretch of wilderness has killed 2,100 people. But in the last four years, the body count has really taken off, soaring to dizzying new highs.

The Mexican Drug War and the cartels involved have been known to have taken away almost 50,000 lives in the past six years. Children have been employed in hazardous situations and are denied basic rights and freedoms, the mental and physical health of the workers are being affected and the country’s resources are being drained. This committee should hope to end the war against the drug cartels in victory, which the Latin American Commission on Drugs and Democracy had declared a “failure” in 2009.

The mandate of the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights allows the analysis and investigation of individual petitions by the public in which violations of human rights are alleged to have been committed by one of the countries present in the committee. Moreover, the Commission could hold a temporary session of the Inter-American Court of Human Rights when two countries or blocs instigate a challenge against each other when they have different viewpoints, in which the Chair will have the final say in the ruling.

It must also be noted here that this stimulation of IACHR will not stick merely to the agenda of illegal migration and drug trafficking, but will delve into other humanitarian issues in the Inter- American continent, such as the Civil War in Columbia and its consequences on basic human rights, the rising starvation rates in Guatemala, Nicaragua, El Salvador and Honduras due to drought, sex trafficking in Mexico, the reports of war crimes and crimes against humanity in Guatemala, the plight of the internally displaced Native Tribes across the continent and so forth, all of which shall be mentioned in the background guide, and may be introduced as a crises.
